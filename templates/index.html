Police Complaint Data
While Philadelphia’s public complaint data hides the identities of those involved, the records include the race and gender of the person filing the complaint and the officer involved.
Complaint Investigations
In Philadelphia, the complaint is investigated by the police department’s own Internal Affairs division. In some cities, these investigations are conducted by civilian oversight boards.

But in the case of this complaint, the investigation, like all complaints in Philadelphia, is made behind closed doors and the decision making process is not always clear.

The outcome of the police department’s investigation of Officer MH’s verbal abuse is “No Sustained Findings”, legalese for the department believing there was not sufficient evidence to back up the complaint.
Discarded Complaints
In Philadelphia, the overwhelming majority of cases are dropped by the police department’s Internal Affairs division. In the seven years of available data in Philadelphia, just 16% of complaints have a “Sustained Finding,” which means there is enough evidence to support the complaint.

This aligns with studies done on other cities, where the rate can range from 6% to 20%. While officer complaints in Philadelphia are investigated by police, some evidence exists that investigations involving a civilian oversight board are more likely to result in sustained findings. {# Sustained Finding,#} {# No Sustained Findings, and#} {# Investigation Pending.#}
Disciplinary Outcomes
Sustained complaints, do not immediately result in punishment for officers—cases are first passed to an officer’s commander.

The officer’s commander decides whether it merits minor punishment or may pass the complaint to the Police Board of Inquiry for a disciplinary hearing, conducted by three members of the police department who will make a disciplinary recommendation. Their report is then sent to the Police Commissioner, who makes a final decision on disciplinary action.

Only 2%, or 218 of the 11,107 complaints since 2013, result in a "Guilty Finding" and consequently a reprimand, termination, suspension, or criminal charges (the data doesn’t make any distinctions among these outcomes).
White Complainants
In line with decades of prior research, race plays a large role in predicting guilty outcomes.

For example, when the complaint is made by a white civilian, guilty findings increase from 2% to 3%.
Black Complainants
Complaints by Black civilians are far more common in Philadelphia, but guilty rates drop to just 1.3% (compared to 3% for white civilians), underscoring the disparities in which Black communities are policed and a particular lack of accountability for officer actions.
Black Complainant/White Officer
This guilty finding rate shrinks even further for complaints filed specifically against white officers: just 0.6% result in a guilty finding, or 22 of the 3,698 complaints against white officers filed by Black complainants. White officers are consistently less likely to face disciplinary action than their peers.

Overdue Investigations
The police complaint system often fails to provide meaningful accountability, even when functioning as intended. But in many cases, it doesn’t even function as intended.

According to Philadelphia Law, investigations must be completed by Internal Affairs within seventy-five days of the complaint filing date. Even when complaints are filtered to only include those that are over two years old, there are still nearly 40 pending investigations (or that were still pending as of the city's last public update).
Complaint Classifications
The complaints have been filtered to include only those classified as "Physical Abuse", "Verbal Abuse", "Criminal Allegation", "Sexual Crime/Misconduct", and "Civil Rights Complaint" (charges that many would view as more serious).

Note, by hovering over the section labels, that the rates of both sustained findings and guilty findings are even lower for these types of complaints. Studies involving other departments have found similar patterns.

In the rare cases where these complaints do result in action, it is often not for the original accusation. Among these five complaint types, 52% are re-classified in the investigation process into different, usually less severe, allegations (24% turn into "Departmental Violations").

Police Complaint Data Shows A Broken Accountability Process



In the current wake of police violence, a unifying demand among protests is stronger accountability for officer conduct, including misuse of force. One important part of that accountability is the civilian complaint process, or how citizens can individually report on police behavior.


Police complaints, ranging from physical abuse to unprofessional conduct, rarely result in consequences for officers. Concrete data is rare among departments, but in 2017, the Philadelphia Police Department became one of the few to report on every complaint it received. The patterns found in Philadelphia clarify many of the anecdotal observations across departments throughout the country, especially when it comes to racial disparities (e.g., complaints of White officers by Black civilians).

Philadelphia provides more publicly accessible data on police complaints than almost any other major U.S. city.


Since data was first published online in 2017, the rate of investigations resulting in sustained findings has gone up more than 60% (you can verify for yourself above), yet in the majority of these cases, disciplinary action has remained pending for months or years, and officers attached to multiple complaints remain on the job.


Public data is a first step in holding police departments accountable, but it is not enough. Philadelphia has more data available than other cities, but complaint summaries are redacted, disciplinary outcomes remain vague, and anonymized IDs replace the names of officers with more than a dozen recorded complaints. In order to hold institutions accountable, data must also be transparent.
