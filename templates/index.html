Police Complaint Data Shows A Broken Accountability Process

By Sam Learner

In the current wake of police violence, protestors are demanding a stronger accountability for officer conduct and misuse of force. In many cities, that accountability is partly served by the civilian complaint process, or how citizens can individually report on police behavior.

Police complaints by citizens, ranging from physical abuse to unprofessional conduct, rarely result in consequences for officers. Concrete data is rare among departments, but in 2017, the Philadelphia Police Department became one of the few to report on every officer complaint it received. The patterns found in Philadelphia clarify many of the anecdotal observations across departments throughout the country, especially when it comes to racial disparities (e.g., complaints of White officers by Black civilians).

This is a real complaint about verbal abuse, one among 10,000+ complaints dating back to 2013.

Complaint Summary

The complainant, SD, 46/B/F, states that she was falsely arrested and verbally abused by Officer H, 19th District. According to the complainant, on 4-26-14, at 11:00 AM, she exited a deli with a can of beer. When she stopped to talk to a friend, Officer H called her over to the car and asked, "Don't you think it's kind of early to be drinking?" The complainant said she wasn't drinking and the can wasn't open; Officer H disagreed with her. She informed Officer H that she had just paid a $200.00 fine for an open container of beer in March and showed him a copy of the citation. He laughed, stating, "I wrote this and I'm going to write you another f**king one." The complainant maintains Officer H incorrectly wrote the can size on the citation as being 16 ounces instead of 24 ounces.

While Philadelphia’s public complaint data hides the identities of those involved, the records include the race and gender of the officer and person filing the complaint.
In Philadelphia, the complaint is investigated by the police department’s own Internal Affairs division. In some cities, these investigations are conducted by civilian oversight boards.

But in the case of this complaint, the investigation, like all complaints in Philadelphia, is made behind closed doors and the decision-making process is not always clear.

For this complaint, the police’s investigation of the officer’s alleged verbal abuse is no sustained findings. This is legalese for the police department finding there was not sufficient evidence to back up the citizen’s complaint.
Philadelphia Police Complaint Outcomes
What happens when officers receive complaints from complainants, about incidents in income districts.
In Philadelphia, the police department drops an overwhelming majority of cases: in the seven years of available data, just 16% of complaints have a sustained finding—there is enough evidence to support the complaint.

This aligns with studies from other city police departments, where the rate can range from 6% to 20%. While officer complaints in Philadelphia are investigated by police, some evidence exists that investigations involving a civilian oversight board are more likely to result in sustained findings.
Sustained complaints, do not immediately result in punishment for officers—cases are first passed to an officer’s commander

The officer’s commander decides whether it merits minor punishment or may pass the complaint to the Police Board of Inquiry for a disciplinary hearing, conducted by three members of the police department who make a disciplinary recommendation. Their report is then sent to the Police Commissioner for a final decision on disciplinary action.

Only 2%, or 218 of the 11,107 complaints since 2013, result in a guilty finding and consequently the officer is reprimanded, terminationed, suspended, or criminal charged (the data doesn’t make any distinctions among these outcomes).
In line with decades of prior research, race correlates strongly with guilty outcomes.

For example, when the complaint is made by a white civilian, guilty findings increase from 2% to 3%.
While complaints by Black civilians are far more common in Philadelphia, guilty rates more than halve to 1.3% (compared to 3% for white civilians), underscoring the disparities in which Black communities are policed and a particular lack of accountability for officer actions.
This guilty finding rate shrinks even further for complaints filed against white officers: 22 of the 3,698 complaints against white officers filed by Black complainants, amounting to just 0.6%. White officers are consistently less likely to face disciplinary action than their peers.

As of 2019, Philadelphia began redacting full details of complaints and replacing them with boilerplate 1-2 sentence summaries. Full summaries for complaints before July 2018 are provided, courtesy of Philly Declaration's Police Accountability Project, which published datasets before the city began redacting summaries.
Here, the complaints have been color-coded based on the number of known prior complaints for the involved officer. Note that this tabulation is based only on the complaints dating back to 2013 that have been provided to the public, so these officers could have any number of additional complaints from 2012 or earlier. Like many other cities, a small group of officers in Philadelphia account for a disproportionate number of complaints. Over a dozen officers, recently identified, have been involved in ten or more publicly-disclosed complaints.
The complaints have been filtered to include only "Physical Abuse" complaints, often filed in response to police use of force. As rare as guilty findings are in general, they’re even more rare for these complaints. Just 39 of 2665, or 1.5%, resulted in guilty findings (you can hover over the section labels to confirm). Prior research involving other departments has found similar patterns.

Even when these complaints do result in consequences, it is often not for the original accusation. In 53% of cases, physical abuse complaints are re-classified during the investigation process into different, usually less severe, allegations. A quarter of such reclassified physical abuse complaints end up as "Departmental Violations".

Philadelphia provides more publicly accessible data on police complaints than almost any other major U.S. city.

Since data was first published online in 2017, the rate of investigations resulting in sustained findings has gone up more than 60%, yet in the majority of these cases, disciplinary action has remained pending for months or years, and officers attached to multiple complaints remain on the job.

Public data is a first step in holding police departments accountable, but it is not enough. Philadelphia has more data available than other cities, but complaint summaries are redacted, disciplinary outcomes remain vague, and anonymized IDs replace the names of officers with more than a dozen recorded complaints. In order to hold institutions accountable, data must also be transparent.
